Taking Ownership as a Caregiver

I’ve been caregiving for over ten years, and for most of that time, I relied heavily on advice, guidance, and authority from others— doctors, nurses, caregivers, family, and friends. I thought that if I listened carefully, followed their instructions, and did what “experts” suggested, I would be doing the right thing for my partner and for myself.

For a long time, I felt a kind of comfort in following guidance from others. It gave me a sense of direction and reassurance that I wasn’t making mistakes. But over the years, I noticed something subtle but persistent: many solutions that could have helped us were never suggested, or they didn’t fit our unique situation.

Recently, after months of my partner being housebound due to mobility issues, I had a simple yet powerful idea: a wheelchair. At first, I hesitated to act on it. I kept wondering, “Is this the right thing to do, should I just go with the limitations of the disease? Should a nurse have suggested this?” But then I realized: if I waited for permission or guidance, we might continue struggling unnecessarily.

I was rather surprised—and a little frustrated—that no one had suggested it. Not nurses, not caregivers, not family or friends. And yet, as soon as the wheelchair arrived, our lives changed. Suddenly, my partner could move freely, go outside again, and participate in small activities that had been impossible for months. The difference wasn’t just physical; it was emotional. We both felt a renewed sense of freedom, connection, and possibility.

This experience made me realize an essential truth about caregiving: you are the primary director of your life and the life of the person you care for. Others can offer advice, guidance, or support, but ultimately, it is your awareness, intuition, and initiative that shape your day-to-day reality.

Taking ownership doesn’t mean ignoring guidance—it means listening thoughtfully, then acting based on what makes sense for your unique circumstances. It means being willing to try solutions, even when they haven’t been suggested or when others aren't taking the same path, and being prepared to adapt and learn along the way.

For me, this has been a gentle but profound shift. I’ve started noticing more possibilities for small improvements: an assistive device here, a new routine there, a tiny change that can make a huge difference in quality of life. I’ve learned to trust myself, to pay attention to the subtle signs of strain, discomfort, or frustration, and to ask, “What can I do to make this better?”

To fellow caregivers, my message is this: don’t wait to be told what to do or what you need. Your insight and initiative are vital. You can trust yourself to make decisions based on your unique situation. Observe closely, reflect honestly, and take steps—even small ones—to improve life for both yourself and the person you care for. Sometimes, the solutions are simple and obvious in hindsight, but they require someone to take the first step. That someone can be you.

Caregiving is a delicate balance of attentiveness, patience, and resilience. It is easy to feel dependent on the advice of others or to assume someone else knows better. But in reality, you are the expert in your own life and the life of your loved one. Trust yourself. Notice the possibilities. Act. Even small changes can bring profound improvements to daily life and wellbeing.

In the end, taking ownership is not just about finding solutions—it’s about reclaiming agency, embracing responsibility, and discovering that the caregiver’s role is also a path toward insight, empowerment, and deep connection.
