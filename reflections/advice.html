How to Handle Well-Meaning but Unhelpful Advice from Others

One of the unexpected challenges of caregiving is navigating the advice of others. Friends, family members, and even professionals often want to help, but their words can sometimes land heavily. “You should put him in care.” “You need to take a break.” “If it were me, I’d do it this way.” While these suggestions may be offered with kindness, they can also feel like judgment or dismissal of the path you are walking.

As caregivers, we carry both the intimate knowledge of our loved one and the daily reality of their care. Outsiders, however loving, do not always see the full picture. Their advice may come from fear, from their own experiences, or even from their own discomfort with illness and decline. Recognizing this can help us hold compassion for where their words come from, while also protecting our own heart.

Remember: You Are the Expert

No one knows your loved one the way you do. You see their rhythms, their needs, their subtle cues. While input from others can sometimes offer fresh perspective, it does not override your lived wisdom. Trust that your experience has value.

When Advice Feels Heavy

If a comment stings, pause before reacting. Take a breath and remember: this is about them, not about you failing. You might choose to simply say, “Thank you, I’ll keep that in mind,” and let it pass. You don’t need to justify your choices to everyone.

Creating Boundaries with Compassion

It’s possible to appreciate someone’s intention without following their direction. A gentle boundary might sound like: “I appreciate your concern. What works best for us right now is…” or “Thank you for caring. I’ll consider that when I feel it’s right.”

Finding Support That Feeds You

Not all advice is unhelpful. Sometimes, we receive words that truly lighten our load. The key is learning to discern — noticing what resonates and letting the rest drift away. Trusted mentors, other caregivers, or professionals who understand dementia can provide guidance that feels supportive rather than overwhelming.

A Gentle Reminder

People give advice because they want to ease suffering — theirs, yours, or both. Let their love in, but don’t let it drown out your own inner knowing. Your caregiving path is uniquely yours, and your heart already carries the wisdom you need.
