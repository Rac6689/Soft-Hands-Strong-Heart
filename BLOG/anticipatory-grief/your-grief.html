Your Grief Needs Expression

Grief can feel overwhelming, like a tidal wave that knocks you down before you even see it coming. There have been many times when I’ve tried to share my grief with family or friends, only to see a look of helplessness, worry, or even shock cross their faces. I don’t blame them—my emotions can be intense, and sometimes I don’t even know what to do with them myself. Expecting others to hold such a heavy load is asking a lot.

The Weight of Holding It In

Because of those reactions, I often found myself holding everything inside. I thought it was kinder to deal with the pain on my own, to keep it contained rather than risk overwhelming others. But bottling it up only left me spinning in circles of helplessness and isolation. Grief unexpressed has a way of growing heavier, pressing harder against the heart, demanding to be acknowledged.

Discovering the Power of Expression

When I first considered starting this blog, I questioned whether I had anything meaningful to add. So many people are already living this reality—what could I possibly say that hadn’t been said before? But when I finally began writing, I couldn’t stop. The words poured out of me, everything I had been holding in finding its way onto the page. And to my surprise, I began to feel lighter. Writing became a release, a way to give form to feelings that had been trapped inside.

Finding Your Own Outlet

Expression doesn’t have to take the form of words. It can be a painting, a piece of music, a long run, a dance in the living room, or even a simple walk in nature. Anything that helps move the energy through you and allows those emotions to breathe can be healing. The form doesn’t matter—what matters is giving yourself permission to express in whatever way feels right for you.

Permission to Feel, Permission to Express

Grief asks for space. It asks to be seen, heard, and felt. When you allow yourself to express it—whether privately or publicly, whether through art, writing, movement, or conversation—you create room for healing. It doesn’t take the grief away, but it transforms the way you carry it. You don’t have to be polished or perfect; you only have to be honest with yourself.

Your grief deserves expression. It is not something to be hidden away or endured in silence. By finding ways to let it out, you may discover a sense of freedom, even lightness, in the midst of sorrow. Give yourself permission to feel, to create, and to share—because in doing so, you honor both your pain and your resilience.
