Using Music to Care for and Connect with Someone with Dementia

Music has always been a powerful thread in my life and in my caregiving journey. For my partner and me, it has been both a source of joy and a profound way to connect when words began to fade. Every day, we used to walk along the river and sing prayers together. He taught me all the Sikh prayers from his childhood, and now, even as he is non-verbal, I am able to sing them back to him. It is one of the most precious and beautiful rituals of our day — a bridge to memory, love, and presence.

Instruments that Invite Play and Connection

Music doesn’t always require words. Many instruments are particularly suited for people living with dementia because they can be played intuitively, offering a direct way to express and experience emotion. Some of the instruments I’ve found especially effective include:

  • Drums: Gentle rhythm can be grounding and joyful, inviting movement and shared energy.
  • Handpans: Their soft, resonant tones are calming and can be played intuitively even by someone with limited dexterity.
  • Xylophones, Koshi Chimes and Bells: Clear, melodic sounds that are easy to strike, helping evoke curiosity and engagement.
  • Percussion Instruments: Shakers, tambourines, and small hand drums are wonderful for sensory exploration and rhythmic play.
  • Native Flutes: Their gentle, flowing tones are soothing and can provide a meditative atmosphere.

Listening to Music

Even if your loved one is no longer able to play or sing, listening to music can be incredibly powerful. Music has the ability to calm, uplift, and even invoke deep memories. Certain songs, hymns, or pieces from earlier in life can spark recognition, evoke smiles, or bring a sense of comfort and familiarity. I encourage caregivers to explore music from their loved one’s past and observe how different melodies influence mood and energy.

Sound Healing in Late-Stage Dementia

As dementia progresses, the ways we communicate change. Sound can become a direct channel to the soul. Gentle instruments like the handpan, native flute, or soft drum beats can create a soothing environment, helping both the caregiver and the loved one feel grounded and connected. My partner finds immense calm in watching me play; it is one of the few things that truly soothes him and brings a quiet joy to our shared space.

Movement and Dance

In the earlier stages, dancing together can be a beautiful way to release stress, express joy, and maintain a sense of connection. Even small, gentle movements to familiar music can evoke laughter, shared smiles, and a reminder that, despite the challenges of dementia, moments of play and closeness are always possible.

Music as a Bridge of Love

Music is more than sound; it is a language that transcends words, a way to meet someone where they are, and a tool to nurture the spirit. Whether through singing familiar prayers, playing instruments together, listening to cherished songs, or sharing a gentle rhythm, music creates presence and deepens connection. In caregiving, it reminds us that love and communication are not limited to words — they live in sound, rhythm, and the heart.

If you are walking this path with someone living with dementia, consider how music might become a bridge for you, offering connection, comfort, and moments of shared joy.
