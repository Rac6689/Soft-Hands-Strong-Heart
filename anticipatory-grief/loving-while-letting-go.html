Loving While Letting Go

One of the most tender and complicated parts of caregiving is living with a slow goodbye. With dementia, the changes don’t happen all at once. They come gradually, like waves pulling someone you love further and further from shore. You can still see them, still touch them, still love them—yet in quiet ways, you are already beginning to grieve.

My own journey with grief has mirrored the progression of the disease. For long stretches, things would feel steady—we’d find our rhythm, adjust to a “new normal,” and life would tick along. And then, suddenly, a change would come that hit me like a bus. The beginning of incontinence, the loss of words, declining mobility, swallowing issues, pressure sores—each new stage was deeply confronting. Not only did I have to find practical ways to cope with the changes, but I also had to grieve the loss of the life we had just adjusted to. Those grief waves often lasted months as I tried to make sense of our new reality. Sometimes I distracted myself with research, endlessly searching for solutions. And yet, grief would always find me—often in the quiet of the car, driving a long stretch of road, tears spilling as my partner sat beside me. No matter how much we try to push it down, grief has a way of slipping in through the cracks.

Finding Love in the Present

Even as memory slips away, love remains. I’ve discovered that small gestures—holding hands, sharing music, sitting in silence together—can carry more weight than words. Love doesn’t always need to be remembered to be real. Sometimes, it’s enough that it exists in the moment, a quiet reminder that connection is still possible.

Honoring the Person They Were

Part of loving while letting go is remembering the life that came before. I find comfort in sharing stories, looking through photos, or even cooking meals that carry old memories. These rituals honor the whole person—past and present—and remind me that even though things are changing, their essence hasn’t disappeared.

Letting Go in Small, Gentle Ways

One way I’ve found to let go, without feeling like I’m abandoning hope, is by gently decluttering as we go. When something is no longer needed, I release it. This keeps me from being surrounded by clutter and from holding on to things that only represent a past chapter we can’t return to. Making space for where we are now feels like an act of love—it honors our present reality and frees me from the weight of false hope. Letting go doesn’t have to be sudden or harsh; it can be a gradual, tender practice that mirrors the changes unfolding day by day.

Allowing Grief to Coexist with Care

It’s tempting to push grief aside, to stay strong and keep going. But I’ve learned that allowing myself to feel the sorrow—even in small, quiet moments—makes me more present and compassionate. Caregiving is not just about tending to another’s needs; it’s also about tending to your own heart as it breaks open and learns to stretch wider.

Preparing Without Losing Today

Anticipatory grief sometimes whispers of the future, of the inevitable loss that’s coming. While that awareness is painful, it also brings clarity: today is precious. Each smile, each moment of connection, each breath we share is something I will one day hold close. Knowing this makes me more grateful, even in the midst of sorrow.

Loving while letting go is not about choosing one or the other. It’s about holding both at once—loving fully in the present while gently releasing your grasp on what used to be. It is a paradox, but also a profound teacher. It shows us the depth of human resilience, the beauty of love that adapts, and the sacredness of every fleeting moment.

If you’re walking this path, know that you are not alone. Grief is not a sign of weakness—it is a sign of love. And in that love, even in the midst of loss, there is something eternal that cannot be taken away.
